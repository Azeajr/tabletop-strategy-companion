## Axis B — Prose & delivery (the UX of comprehension)

Conditions are clear and well under the 45-char cap (max observed: "Opponent setting up a forced jump" = 33 chars). Stealth lines are well-formed (max observed: 39 chars, all ≤50). Each line stands alone and reads at a glance.

| # | location | Finding | Severity | Disposition |
|---|----------|---------|:--------:|-------------|
| B1 | Early Game ▸ Forced Jumps ▸ reading order | Alphabetical condition sort renders "Opponent setting..." (defensive) before "You can set up..." (offensive). A new player learning the phase would intuitively ask "how do I set up a jump?" first, then "how do I defend?" — the sort inverts this. | 🟡 | Recommend-only |
| B2 | Opening ▸ category order | Alphabetical sort renders "King Row Defense" before "Opening Development" even though the opening move (11-15 center play) is the actual first decision. A player reads "how do I open?" before "how do I protect the back row?" — alphabetical order puts the cart before the horse. | 🟡 | Recommend-only |
| B3 | Mid-Game ▸ category order | "King Promotion" is TLDR (highest-value) but alphabetical category sort renders it LAST (Defense → Exchange Strategy → King Promotion). The TLDR hoist works *within* a category but not across categories; the highest-value phase insight is buried. | 🟡 | Recommend-only |
| B4 | aggregate | Stealth collapsed-row density per phase: Opening (2) + Early Game (5) + Mid-Game (2) + End-Game (3). Max is 5 in Early Game, well under the 8-row budget. No density/clipping risk. | 🟢 | Accept |
| B5 | all phases | Detailed prose is crisp, leads with action, and stays in register with stealth. No vagueness, no jargon without definition, no walls of text. | 🟢 | Accept |

**Assessment: All findings B1–B3 are *ordering* issues that require the `order` field (schema §6) to fix. They are NOT content problems — the advice is well-written. The model sorts alphabetically; the game's decision flow doesn't. This is recommend-only.**
